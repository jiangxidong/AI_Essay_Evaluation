Q1

Analyze the primary factors that led to the decline of the feudal system in Europe.

The decline of the feudal system was a multifaceted process driven by economic shifts, such as the growth of a market economy and the rise of towns, which offered peasants alternative livelihoods. The Black Death also played a crucial role by drastically reducing the population, thereby increasing the value of labor and giving serfs more bargaining power. Furthermore, the consolidation of monarchical power and the development of professional armies reduced the military necessity of feudal lords.

Q2

Discuss the impact of the printing press on the Renaissance and the Protestant Reformation.

Gutenberg's printing press revolutionized communication, enabling the rapid dissemination of ideas. During the Renaissance, it facilitated the spread of classical texts and humanist thought. For the Reformation, it was indispensable; Martin Luther's Ninety-five Theses and other works were printed and distributed widely, challenging the Catholic Church's authority and allowing reformist ideas to reach a mass audience, which would have been impossible previously.

Q3

Explain the concept of 'mercantilism' and its role in European colonization.

Mercantilism was an economic theory that a nation's wealth was measured by its holdings of gold and silver. This led to policies aimed at maximizing exports and minimizing imports. Colonies played a critical role by providing raw materials to the mother country and serving as captive markets for its manufactured goods. This system created a flow of wealth from the colonies to Europe, driving the expansion of colonial empires and leading to frequent conflicts between rival European powers.

Q4

Describe the main artistic innovations of the High Renaissance, citing at least two key artists.

The High Renaissance saw artists achieve a new level of realism and emotional depth. Key innovations included the mastery of perspective, the use of sfumato (smoky, soft outlines) by Leonardo da Vinci to create lifelike figures in works like the "Mona Lisa," and the powerful, dynamic anatomy seen in Michelangelo's sculptures, such as his "David." These techniques created a sense of harmony, proportion, and idealized naturalism that defined the period's art.
